Section 1.1 Piecewise-Defined Functions

Â¶

Before we defined the absolute value function, it is extremely important that you understand the concept of a piecewise-defined function . However, let's begin by looking at the graph of a constant function.

Note that no matter what number you substitute for \(x \) in \(f \text{,}\) the output of the function is constantly equal to \(3 \text{.}\) Thus,

\begin{equation*} f(-5) = 3, \; f(0) = 3, \; f(\sqrt{2}) = 3, \; \text{or} \; f(\pi) = 3 \text{.} \end{equation*}

This leads us to the following definition.

Definition 1.1.2.
The function defined by \(f(x) = c\text{,}\) where \(c \) is a constant (fixed real number), is called a constant function.

Two comments are in order:

  1. \(f(x) = 3 \) for all real numbers \(x \text{.}\)

  2. The graph of \(f(x) = 3 \) is a horizontal line. It consists of all points (x,y) having the y-value equal to \(c\text{.}\)

Subsection 1.1.1 Piecewise Constant Functions

Let's look at the following example.

Example 1.1.3. Piecewise Constant Functions.

During the course of the day, there is a need to adjust the light intensity of your cell-phone. You always have the option of gradually increasing your light intensity or jump from low to high intensity (resp. high to low intensity). Lets us consider the latter first.

Suppose we wanted to graph the light intensity recordings of Jonathan's cell-phone during the course of the day. Have a look at the following graph and try to explain what actions did Jonathan perform in altering his cell-phone light intensity.

Between point A and B Jon is busy studying so his cell-phone is off. All of a sudden Mike, his friend shares an Instagram post with him. Simultaneously, as he hears the notification tone (bad library practice, put it on silent) his phone lights up with some intensity recording \(y_{1} \) at point C. He then views this Instagram post (focus Jon, focus) for some time and places his phone down at some point between point C and D. After some time at point D of in-activeness of the cell-phone, the light intensity drops to \(y_{2} \text{,}\) remains illuminated for a bit and then switches off at time \(t_{3} \) (point G) and remains in this state thereafter.

The above gives an easy yet insightfully example of how one can end up having a piecewise constant function.

As an exercise. Construct your own piecewise constant function and attach a story into it. There are not specific rules or mathematical rigor required. Just a simple example like the one above.

Obviously, you won't always have to give stories in order to construct piecewise constant function, we need a more mathematically rigorous way to do this. As a motivation to the next section SubsectionÂ 1.1.2, let us try and provide a possible mathematically rigorous way to describe the above piecewise constant function.

Note that the function takes on values for \(t \gt 0\text{.}\) Between points A and B the function takes on the value \(0 \) then between points C and D it takes on the value \(y_{1} \) and then the value \(y_{2} \) between E and F and then lastly the value \(0 \) for \(t \gt x_{i} \) at point G. Also note that here, \(y_{1} \gt y_{2} \text{.}\)

Now we can write this more formally as:

\begin{equation} p(t) := \begin{cases} f(t) \amp \text{for} \; 0 \leq t \lt t_{1} \\ g(t) \amp \text{for} \; t_{1} \leq t \lt t_{2} \\ h(t) \amp \text{for} \; t_{2} \leq t \lt t_{3} \\ i(t) \amp \text{for} \; t \geq t_{3} \end{cases}\label{eq1}\tag{1.1.1} \end{equation}

Here, \(f = 0, g = y_{1}, h = y_{2} \; and \; i = 0 \) and thus we can write \(p(t) \) as just:

\begin{equation} p(t) := \begin{cases} f(t) = 0 \amp \text{for} \; 0 \leq t \lt t_{1} \\ g(t) = y_{1} \amp \text{for} \; t_{1} \leq t \lt t_{2} \\ h(t) = y_{2} \amp \text{for} \; t_{2} \leq t \lt t_{3} \\ i(t) = 0 \amp \text{for} \; t \geq t_{3} \end{cases}\text{.}\label{eq2}\tag{1.1.2} \end{equation}

However, equation (1.1.2) can look a bit more detailed than necessary so we will usually write it as:

\begin{equation} p(t) := \begin{cases} 0 \amp \text{for} \; 0 \leq t \lt t_{1} \\ y_{1} \amp \text{for} \; t_{1} \leq t \lt t_{2} \\ y_{2} \amp \text{for} \; t_{2} \leq t \lt t_{3} \\ 0 \amp \text{for} \; t \geq t_{3} \end{cases}\text{.}\label{eq3}\tag{1.1.3} \end{equation}

The very important idea we are trying to get across with equation (1.1.1) is that \(p \) is piecewise defined by other functions, namely \(f, \; g, \; h, \; and \; i \text{.}\) However, the functions do not necessarily have to be constant functions only. They can be any functions you can imagine, the only strict restriction is that they must be defined on the domain you would have specified.

Motivated by the above, we can for instance define a function \(m \) as follows:

\begin{equation} m(t) := \begin{cases} f(t) \amp = t \amp \text{for} \; 0 \leq t \lt 2 \\ g(t) \amp = -t + 4 \amp \text{for} \; 2 \leq t \lt 4 \\ h(t) \amp = (x-4)^2 \amp \text{for} \; 4 \leq t \lt 6 \\ i(t) \amp = 4 \amp \text{for} \; t \geq 6 \end{cases}\text{.}\label{eq4}\tag{1.1.4} \end{equation}
