Section 2.7 Multiplication and de Moivre's Theorem

Â¶

Remember when we first saw the basic arithmetic operations on complex numbers? Addition in Cartesian form was really easy, but multiplication, which had this lovely geometric interpretation (it's just rotations) was not so simple. Remember in Cartesian form: \((a+bi)(c+di)=(ac-bd)+(ad+bc)i\) which is just a bit messy. It turns out that in modulus argument form, multiplication is super simple!

Theorem 2.7.1. (a): Multiplication of two complex numbers in the modulus-argument form.

Suppose that \(z= |z|(\cos{\alpha}+i\sin{\alpha})\) and \(w=|w|(\cos{\beta}+i\sin{\beta})\) are non-zero complex numbers in the modulus-argument form, then their product is:

\begin{equation*} zw=|z||w|\Big(\cos{(\alpha+\beta)}+i\sin{(\alpha+\beta)}\Big) \end{equation*}

Remark: This theorem gives us the geometric interpretation for multiplication of complex numbers, i.e. multiplying two complex numbers is equivalent to multiplying their moduli and adding their arguments.

The proof of the theorem is as follows:

Proof.
\begin{align*} zw\amp =\Big(|z|(\cos{\alpha}+i\sin{\alpha})\Big)\Big(|w|(\cos{\beta}+i\sin{\beta})\Big) \textbf{Modulus-argument form }\\ \amp =|z||w|\Big(\cos{\alpha}+i\sin{\alpha}\Big)\Big(\cos{\beta}+i\sin{\beta}\Big) \textbf{Commutative property }\\ \amp =|z||w|\Big( \cos{\alpha}\cos{\beta}+i\cos{\alpha}\sin{\beta}+i\cos{\beta}\sin{\alpha}-\sin{\alpha}\sin{\beta}\Big) \textbf{Definition 2.1(b)}\\ \amp =|z||w|\Big( (\cos{\alpha}\cos{\beta}-\sin{\alpha}\sin{\beta})+i(\cos{\alpha}\sin{\beta}+\cos{\beta}\sin{\alpha})\Big) \textbf{Definition 2.1(a)}\\ \amp =|z||w|\Big(\cos{(\alpha+\beta)}+i\sin{(\alpha+\beta)}\Big) \textbf{Compound angle identities}\Box \end{align*}

and visually, we can see an example of the product of two complex numbers here:

This is something really fundamental to understand and remember: In modulus argument form, when you multiply together two complex numbers, the moduli multiply and the arguments add.

How would you write down the complex number \(i\) in modulus argument form?

Well, it sits on the imaginary axis, and therefore has principle argument \(\frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\) Thus:

\begin{equation*} i=1\left(\cos(\frac{\pi}{2})+i\sin(\frac{\pi}{2})\right) \end{equation*}

This means that multiplying a complex number \(z=|z|(\cos\alpha+i\sin\alpha)\) by \(i\) can be written as:

\begin{equation*} zi=|z|(\cos(\alpha+\frac{\pi}{2})+i\sin(\alpha+\frac{\pi}{2})) \end{equation*}

ie multiplication by \(i\) is equivalent to rotation by \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) in the complex plane...which is one of the ways that we first came across \(i\text{.}\)

Subsection 2.7.1 Taking powers of complex numbers

Now we know how easy it is to multiply together two complex numbers when they are written in modulus-argument form. How about squaring a complex number. This is just a special case of multiplication, where the number is being multiplied by itself. Let's start with complex numbers of modulus 1, and adapt to the more general case later. Take the above expression for \(zw\) and make \(w\) be the same as \(z\text{.}\) ie. if \(z=w=\cos{\theta}+i\sin{\theta}\text{,}\) you get:

\begin{equation*} z^2=\cos{(2\theta)}+i\sin{(2\theta)} \end{equation*}

How about \(z^3\text{?}\) Well, you just multiply \(z^2\) by \(z\text{,}\) which yields:

\begin{equation*} z^3=\cos{(3\theta)}+i\sin{(3\theta)} \end{equation*}

In fact, repeated multiplications like this give us a general statement and the name of it is De Moivre's Theorem:

Theorem 2.7.4. (b): De Moivre's Theorem.

For any integer \(n\) and real number \(\theta\text{,}\)

\((\cos{\theta}+i\sin{\theta})^n=\cos{(n\theta)}+i\sin{(n\theta)}\)

From the picture of multiplication of complex numbers as rotations, de Moivre's theorem should become relatively intuitive. Note that De Moivre's Theorem is for complex numbers with modulus 1. However, the theorem can be extended to include any complex number \(z\) with \(|z|=r\) i.e. \(z=r(\cos{\theta}+i\sin{\theta})\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} z^n=(r(\cos{\theta}+i\sin{\theta}))^n=r^n(\cos{(n\theta)}+i\sin{(n\theta)}) \end{equation*}

Let's look at the proof of De Moivre's Theorem.

De Moivre's Theorem proof:

We use mathematical induction to prove the theorem.

Case 1 : \(n \geq 0\)

For any integer \(n\geq 0\) let \(P(n)\) be the proposition that

\begin{equation*} (\cos{\theta}+i\sin{\theta})^n=\cos{(n\theta)}+i\sin{(n\theta)} \end{equation*}

Base case: The statement \(P(0)\) says that

\begin{equation*} (\cos{\theta}+i\sin{\theta})^0=\cos{(0)}+i\sin{(0)}=1 \end{equation*}

which is true.

Induction Hypothesis (IH): We now assume that \(P(m)\) is true for some non-negative integer \(m\)

\begin{equation*} (\cos{\theta}+i\sin{\theta})^m=\cos{(m\theta)}+i\sin{(m\theta)} \end{equation*}

Inductive Step: It now remains for us to show that \(P(m+1)\) holds true

\begin{align*} \cos{((m+1)\theta)}+i\sin{((m+1)\theta)}\amp = (\cos{\theta}+i\sin{\theta})^{m+1} \\ \amp =(\cos{\theta}+i\sin{\theta})^m(\cos\theta +i\sin\theta)\\ \amp =(\cos{(m\theta)}+i\sin{(m\theta)})(\cos\theta +i\sin\theta) \textbf{IH}\\ \amp =\cos{((m+1)\theta)}+i\sin{((m+1)\theta)} \textbf{theorem} \knowl{./knowl/theorem-4-a.html}{\text{Theorem 2.7.1}} \end{align*}

So \(P(m+1)\) holds true. By the principle of mathematical induction \(P(n)\) is true for all non-negative integers \(n\text{.}\) \end{theorem}

Case 2: \(n \lt 0\)

Let \(n=-k\) where \(n\) is any negative integer, so \(k\) is a positive integer.

\begin{align*} (\cos\theta +i\sin\theta)^n\amp =(\cos\theta +i\sin\theta)^n\\ \amp =(\cos\theta +i\sin\theta)^{-k}\\ \amp =\dfrac1{(\cos\theta +i\sin\theta)^k}\\ \amp =\dfrac1{\cos{(k\theta)} +i\sin{(k\theta})} \amp \textbf{Case 1}\\ \amp =\dfrac1{\cos{(k\theta)} +i\sin{(k\theta})} \times \dfrac{{\cos{(k\theta)} -i\sin{(k\theta})}}{{\cos{(k\theta)} -i\sin{(k\theta})}}\\ \amp =\dfrac{\cos{(k\theta)} -i\sin{(k\theta)}}{\cos^2{(k\theta)} -i^2\sin^2{(k\theta)}}\\ \amp =\dfrac{\cos{(k\theta)} -i\sin{(k\theta)}}{\cos^2{(k\theta)} +\sin^2{(k\theta)}}\\ \amp =\cos{(k\theta)} -i\sin{(k\theta)}\\ \amp =\cos{(-n\theta)} -i\sin{(-n\theta)}\\ \amp =\cos{(n\theta)} +i\sin{(n\theta)} \end{align*}

We have now proved that \((\cos\theta +i\sin\theta)^n=(\cos\theta +i\sin\theta)^n\) for any integer \(n\text{.}\) \(\Box\)

Example 2.7.5.

  1. Use De Moivre's Theorem to write \((1+i)^{20}\) in a cartesian from.

  2. Write \((\sqrt{3}+i)^3(1+\sqrt{3}i)^5\) in a modulus-argument form.

  3. Write \(\dfrac{(1+i)^{10}}{1+\sqrt{3}i}\) in a modulus-argument form.

Solution

  1. We first need to write this number in modulus argument as that is how de Moivre's theorem is stated. We can write:

    \begin{equation*} (1+i)=\sqrt{2}(\cos\frac{\pi}{4}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{4}) \end{equation*}

    and therefore:

    \begin{align*} (1+i)^{20}\amp = (\sqrt{2}(\cos\frac{\pi}{4}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{4}))^{20}=\sqrt{2}^{20}(\cos\frac{20\pi}{4}+i\sin\frac{20\pi}{4})\nonumber\\ \amp = 2^{10}(\cos 5\pi+i\sin 5\pi)\nonumber \end{align*}

    But we know that we can always subtract any integer multiple of \(2\pi\) from an argument and it will still be the same complex number. Because the angle here is \(5\pi\) if we subtract 2 lots of \(2\pi\) we will end up with \(\pi\) which is a principle argument, so:

    \begin{equation*} (1+i)^{20}=2^{10}(\cos \pi+i\sin \pi)=2^{10}(-1+0 i)=-2^{10} \end{equation*}

  2. We will do the same thing for the next example, but without the explanatory steps. Go through and make sure that you can see what is happening here:

    \begin{align*} (\sqrt{3}+i)^3(1+\sqrt{3}i)^5\amp = (2(\cos(\frac{\pi}{6}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{6}))^3((2(\cos\frac{\pi}{3}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{3}))^5\nonumber\\ \amp = 2^3(\cos\frac{3\pi}{6}+i\sin\frac{3\pi}{6})2^5(\cos\frac{5\pi}{3}+i\sin\frac{5\pi}{3})\nonumber\\ \amp = 2^32^5(\cos(\frac{3\pi}{6}+\frac{10\pi}{6})+i\sin(\frac{3\pi}{6}+\frac{10\pi}{6})\nonumber\\ \amp = 2^8(\cos\frac{13\pi}{6}+i\sin\frac{13\pi}{6})\nonumber\\ \amp = 2^8(\cos\frac{\pi}{6}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{6})\nonumber\\ \amp = 2^8(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}+\frac{i}{2})=2^7(\sqrt{3}+i)\nonumber \end{align*}

  3. And again, make sure that you can follow all steps:

    \begin{align*} \frac{(1+i)^{10}}{1+\sqrt{3}i}\amp = (1+i)^{10}(1+\sqrt{3}i)^{-1}\nonumber\\ \amp = (\sqrt{2}(\cos\frac{\pi}{4}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{4}))^{10} (2(\cos\frac{\pi}{3}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{3}))^{-1}\nonumber\\ \amp = \frac{2^5}{2}(\cos\frac{10\pi}{4}+i\sin\frac{10\pi}{4})(\cos(-\frac{\pi}{3})+i\sin(-\frac{\pi}{3}))\nonumber\\ \amp = 2^4(\cos(\frac{10\pi}{4}-\frac{pi}{3})+i\cos(\frac{10\pi}{4}-\frac{pi}{3}))\nonumber\\ \amp = 2^4(\cos(\frac{13\pi}{6})+i\cos(\frac{13\pi}{6}))\nonumber\\ \amp = 16(\sqrt{3}{2}+\frac{i}{2})=8(\sqrt{3}+i) \end{align*}
